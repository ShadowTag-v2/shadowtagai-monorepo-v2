A: Your “Contractual” app concept directly addresses both core issues you’ve identified, though with varying degrees of effectiveness:

**Strong Solutions to Your Stated Problems:**

For **informal agreements**, Contractual creates exactly what you’re missing - a signed, documented record. Instead of relying on “memory under oath,” you’d have actual evidence of what was discussed and agreed to. This is particularly powerful for small claims court, where judges often struggle with he-said/she-said disputes.

For **ambiguous terms**, the AI organizing feature that sorts “competing terms into opposing lists” could force clarity during negotiation. When parties see their conflicting interpretations laid out, they’re more likely to define specifics upfront rather than leave things vague.

**Practical Strengths:**

The transmission shop scenario you describe is spot-on. Currently, when they quote you one price verbally then charge another, you have no admissible evidence of the original quote. Contractual would capture that initial conversation with their signature on it. Game-changer for small disputes.

The geo-location feature for jurisdiction-appropriate language is smart - contract law varies significantly by state, and small claims procedures definitely do.

**Potential Challenges:**

Getting the *other party* to participate is your biggest hurdle. Why would a shop owner sign something that limits their ability to later claim “miscommunication”? You’d need compelling value propositions for both sides - perhaps emphasizing dispute prevention rather than evidence creation.

The “practice of law” concern you mention is worth examining carefully. While documenting agreements isn’t practicing law, having AI “analyze contracts for legal issues” might trigger regulatory scrutiny in some states.

**Market Reality Check:**

Your core insight about the $20k lawyer threshold is absolutely correct. There’s a real justice gap for smaller disputes. However, the businesses causing these problems often benefit from the current ambiguous system. Adoption might be slow unless there’s regulatory pressure or customer demand forces it.

The app solves a genuine problem that affects millions of people annually. Success would depend heavily on execution and getting that crucial two-sided adoption.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​

**AI Integration Strategy:**

**Partner with existing AI (recommended):**

- Use established APIs like OpenAI, Anthropic Claude, or Google’s models
- Much faster to market (months vs years)
- Lower upfront costs ($10K-50K vs $500K-5M+)
- Proven reliability and legal compliance
- You retain 100% of app revenue, just pay API costs (typically $0.01-0.10 per interaction)

**Training your own LLM:**

- Requires massive datasets, compute resources, and ML expertise
- 12-24 month development timeline minimum
- High risk of inferior performance compared to established models
- Only makes sense if you have $5M+ budget and specific legal training data others lack

**Revenue doesn’t get “split” with AI providers** - you pay usage fees like any other service (hosting, payment processing, etc.). Think Netflix paying AWS for servers, not sharing Netflix subscription revenue.

**Optimal Revenue Model for Contractual:**

**Freemium SaaS with transaction fees:**

*Free Tier:*

- Basic contract documentation (3 per month)
- Standard templates
- Builds user base and demonstrates value

*Premium Subscriptions:*

- Individual: $9.99/month (unlimited contracts, AI analysis)
- Business: $29.99/month (multi-user, custom templates, priority support)
- Enterprise: $99+/month (white-label, advanced analytics, API access)

**High-margin add-ons:**

- Online notarization: $25-50 per document (huge margins after setup)
- Legal referral fees: 5-15% of lawyer referral fees
- Expedited processing: $10-25 premium
- Document storage/retrieval: $5/month for extended history

**Why this model works:**

- Recurring revenue provides stability
- Transaction fees scale with usage
- Legal services traditionally command premium pricing
- B2B customers will pay significantly more than consumers

The notarization feature alone could generate $50-100K monthly once you have modest scale.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​
