Phase 1 vs. Own-Hardware: Detailed Proposal (with ROI + “ETF-style” views)

TL;DR
	•	Phase 1 (cloud GPU partners) wins when your effective price per H100 GPU-hour is ≤ $3–$5 or your utilization < ~25–30%.
	•	Owning DGX/Blackwell wins when utilization is high (≥ ~80% at $3/hr; ≥ ~25% at $10/hr) and you can keep the cluster busy with production workloads (fine-tuning, inference, RAG re-ranking, video, agents).

⸻

Assumptions used (transparent and adjustable)
	•	Target cluster: 64× H100-class GPUs (think 8× DGX nodes with 8 GPUs each).
	•	Cloud per-GPU hour (two price bands you’re likely to see):
	•	$10/hr (hyperscaler on-demand equivalent)
	•	$3/hr (specialist GPU providers / committed discounts)
	•	On-prem CapEx: $3.2M for 8× DGX H100 8-GPU nodes (midpoint incl. high-speed fabric).
	•	Depreciation: 36 months straight-line.
	•	Power: ~10 kW per DGX → 80 kW total; electricity $0.12/kWh; cooling +40% of power (PUE≈1.4).
	•	Facilities/support: $15k/month (net+admin+warranty).
(Optional) NVIDIA AI Enterprise licenses not included; add if you plan to rely on NIM/enterprise stack.

Phase 1 (Cloud-based GPU via partners): plan & why it’s right now

What we do
	1.	Multi-cloud portfolio (on-demand + reserved + spot):
	•	Hyperscalers for burst & enterprise controls.
	•	Specialist GPU providers for low $/GPU-hr lanes (preemptible/committed).
	2.	Workload routing:
	•	Fine-tuning, long-context training → cheapest reliable lane.
	•	Real-time inference → low-latency zones (regional replicas).
	3.	Built-in FinOps + SLOs:
	•	Track $/GPU-hr, GPU-util%, queue time, job success, energy per token.
	4.	Continuous Delivery for Models:
	•	CI/CD (GitHub Actions) promoting from dev→staging→prod with automatic rollback.

Why now
	•	No CapEx; instant elasticity; lets us discover real utilization and true cost curves before committing to racks.
	•	Ideal while Growth Engine demand is ramping and volatile.

⸻

Own Hardware (DGX / Blackwell) when it makes sense

What we buy
	•	8× DGX-class nodes (64 GPUs) or a Blackwell rack when available (higher density & perf/Watt).
	•	Infiniband/NVLink fabric, tiered NVMe storage, reliable power/cooling.

When it wins
	•	Stable, high utilization (≥ ~25% at $10/hr, ≥ ~82% at $3/hr).
	•	Sensitive data/locality constraints; predictable long-running training; need deterministic throughput.

Key risks
	•	Under-utilization, staffing for ops, parts lead-times, and technical debt if we scale too quickly.

“ETF-Style” Comparisons (diversified baskets)

1) Cloud Compute Access ETF (C-ETF)

A diversified “basket” to reduce price and capacity risk.
	•	Constituents & indicative weights (tune monthly by cost/latency):
	•	30% Hyperscaler A (enterprise guardrails, reserved discounts)
	•	25% Specialist Provider 1 (low $/H100-hr, spot commitments)
	•	20% Hyperscaler B (regional redundancy)
	•	15% Specialist Provider 2 (overflow bursts)
	•	10% Hyperscaler C (edge/latency PoPs)
	•	Return driver: lower blended $/GPU-hr; higher availability; fast capacity.
	•	Risk: preemptions (mitigate via checkpointing), egress fees, variable queues.

2) On-Prem Infra ETF (O-ETF)

A basket of on-prem components to spread TCO risk.
	•	Constituents & indicative weights:
	•	55% NVIDIA systems (DGX/HGX/Blackwell nodes)
	•	15% High-speed fabric (InfiniBand/NVLink switches/links)
	•	15% NVMe tiers (fast scratch + object)
	•	10% Facility upgrades (power, cooling, monitoring)
	•	5% Ops toolchain (or NVIDIA AI Enterprise)
	•	Return driver: high utilization → very low marginal cost per token/epoch.
	•	Risk: utilization shortfalls, ops complexity, stranded capital.
