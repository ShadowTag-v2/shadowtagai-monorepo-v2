## Inference optimization achieves step-function improvements

vLLM V1 alpha released January 27, 2025, delivers **1.7x throughput improvement** over V0 through a rewritten execution loop with near-zero CPU overhead. The architecture integrates FlashAttention 3 for mixed prefill and decode batches, zero-overhead prefix caching with less than 1% degradation at 0% hit rates, and enhanced multimodal support for Qwen2-VL and LLaVA models. PagedAttention improvements maintain non-contiguous KV cache with 16-token block-based allocation achieving less than 4% memory fragmentation through just-in-time allocation. The system supports 100+ model architectures spanning LLMs, MoE variants, and vision models with hardware compatibility across NVIDIA H100/A100, AMD MI300X, Google TPU v5e/v6e, AWS Inferentia, and Intel Gaudi.

Aegaeon GPU pooling from Alibaba Cloud remains proprietary despite publication at SOSP 2025, with no open-source release. The research demonstrates token-level auto-scaling reducing 1,192 GPUs to 213 for equivalent workload—an 82% reduction. The innovation enables preemptive model switching during generation supporting 7 models per GPU versus 2-3 for alternatives with 97% reduction in auto-scaling overhead. Production deployments show 2-2.5x higher request rates versus ServerlessLLM, but organizations seeking similar capabilities must turn to open alternatives.

Open-source alternatives to Aegaeon center on **NVIDIA NIM for production-ready microservices** with TensorRT-LLM optimization and OpenAI-compatible APIs. Triton Inference Server provides multi-framework serving supporting vLLM, TensorRT-LLM, and PyTorch with robust production deployment patterns. KServe offers Kubernetes-native model serving with autoscaling and multi-model support. The ServerlessLLM and MuxServe academic projects provide research-grade implementations limited to 2-3 models per GPU without the sophisticated token-level scheduling of Aegaeon.

Ray 2.51.1 released November 2025 with Ray Serve providing first-class vLLM integration and native LLM APIs. The platform enables OpenAI-compatible routing with autoscaling supporting fractional GPU allocation and multi-model serving composition. Integration patterns span tensor parallelism splitting models across GPUs, pipeline parallelism distributing layers across nodes, data parallelism with multiple replicas, and expert parallelism for MoE architectures. Production deployments include DeepSeek-R1 on GKE with 16 H100 GPUs and multi-region inference clusters with hybrid batch and online serving.

Token-level scheduling implementations span continuous batching as the industry standard through vLLM, TGI, TensorRT-LLM, and SGLang delivering 8-23x throughput versus static batching. Chunked prefill from Sarathi-Serve splits prompts into chunks interleaved with decode operations preventing decode stalls with 2-5x p99 latency improvements. The 2025 layered prefill research for MoE models partitions along layer dimensions achieving 39% memory traffic reduction with 70% lower time-to-first-token and 22% energy savings. Recent research on throughput-optimal scheduling, online scheduling with KV cache constraints, and FairBatching demonstrates continued innovation in inference optimization.

Continuous batching optimizations deliver 23x throughput with PagedAttention combined with 8x gains from dynamic batching alone. Iteration latency improvements in vLLM V1 drop from 30-48ms in V0 to 20-31ms representing 30-40% reductions. The persistent batch technique caches tensors and applies diffs rather than recreating batches each iteration. FlashAttention 3 integration provides 2-3x faster attention computation while speculative decoding enables 2-3x generation speedups through draft model verification.

Achieving **sub-90ms p99 latency** requires carefully orchestrated architecture decisions. Model selection favors 0.5-3B parameters achieving under 20ms time-to-first-token and under 15ms time-between-tokens, with 7-8B models at 30-50ms and 20-25ms respectively. FP8 quantization provides 2x speedup with minimal quality degradation. Infrastructure requirements center on H100 GPUs for best performance with A100 acceptable and L4 for budget deployments, noting memory bandwidth criticality for decode operations. Batch sizes must remain at 1-4 for low latency versus larger batches trading latency for throughput. The optimization stack combines FlashAttention 3, FP8 quantization, speculative decoding, prefix caching for system prompts, and CUDA graphs reducing kernel overhead.

GKE deployment modules configure GPU node pools with nvidia-tesla-t4 accelerators, gpu_driver_installation_config for LATEST driver versions, taints ensuring nvidia.com/gpu=present with NO_SCHEDULE effects, and autoscaling with min_node_count at 0 and max_node_count enabling cost-effective burst capacity.

State management best practices require remote backends via GCS or S3 with encryption at rest and versioning, separate workspaces per environment for development, staging, and production isolation, state locking through DynamoDB or native mechanisms preventing concurrent modifications, and drift detection with scheduled tofu plan identifying configuration divergence. The approach enables declarative infrastructure definitions, version control integration, automated deployments, and consistent environments across regions.

Kubernetes 1.34 in rapid channels and **1.33 Octarine stable** released April 2025 with 64 enhancements including 18 stable features and 20 beta features maintains support for the 3 most recent minor versions. ML workload features center on GPU scheduling through Device Plugin Framework supporting nvidia.com/gpu and amd.com/gpu resources, GPU sharing via time-slicing and Multi-Instance GPU, Dynamic Resource Allocation with structured parameters enhanced in v1.33, topology awareness for GPU interconnect-aware scheduling, and KAI Scheduler as NVIDIA's open-source solution providing fractional GPU support, gang scheduling, and bin-packing optimization.

Autoscaling mechanisms span Horizontal Pod Autoscaler scaling based on CPU, memory, and custom metrics, Vertical Pod Autoscaler adjusting resources for memory-intensive ML workloads, and Cluster Autoscaler providing node-level scaling with GPU node pools supporting min=0 for aggressive cost savings. The Kubernetes 1.33 release adds asynchronous preemption in beta improving scheduler performance for high-churn ML workloads, OCI image volumes in beta packaging model weights as OCI artifacts, and custom resource field selectors enabling better filtering for ML custom resource definitions.

Service mesh selection between Istio 1.27.3 and Linkerd 2.18.2 depends on workload characteristics. **Linkerd 2.18 delivers best P99 latency** at 2000 RPS with improvements of 163ms versus Istio sidecar and 11.2ms versus Istio Ambient, data plane memory of approximately 10MB versus 250MB for Istio sidecar and 150MB for Ambient, 10x lower data plane CPU consumption, and Rust implementation versus C++ Envoy proxies. Istio 1.27 provides Gateway API Inference Extension for AI workloads on Kubernetes 1.29-1.33, advanced traffic management for canary deployments, multi-cluster deployments at enterprise scale supporting 100+ clusters, and mTLS with configurable STRICT or PERMISSIVE modes.

Recommendations favor **Linkerd for latency-critical inference** with 10-163ms improvements at scale, resource efficiency optimizing GPU nodes, operational simplicity, and small to medium scale deployments under 100 clusters. Istio suits scenarios requiring Gateway API Inference Extension, enterprise features for sophisticated traffic management, multi-cluster deployments, and large scale exceeding 100 clusters. Both provide automatic mTLS with Linkerd default-on and Istio configurable, load balancing with Linkerd's EWMA versus Istio's multiple algorithms, and Prometheus/Grafana observability integration.

GitOps platforms ArgoCD v3.1.9 and Flux v2.7.2 both released October 2025 provide complementary approaches to continuous delivery. **ArgoCD excels with rich Web UI** supporting project-based multi-tenancy, native OCI support from v3.1, external Image Updater for automation, quarterly releases providing 4x annual updates, and declarative application definitions with automated sync policies. Flux emphasizes CLI-based workflows with optional Weave GitOps, native GA image automation from v2.7, namespace-based multi-tenancy, bootstrap process installation, and minimum 3x annual releases.

Patterns for ML infrastructure deployment include OCI repositories for immutable model artifacts with semantic versioning, image policies with digest pinning preventing upstream mutations, automated rollout strategies spanning blue-green deployments switching traffic atomically, canary with progressive 10% → 50% → 100% rollouts, and A/B testing routing based on headers for model comparison. Rollback strategies in ArgoCD use argocd app rollback with automatic sync while Flux suspends kustomizations, reconciles to specific commit SHAs, and resumes after verification.

Monitoring with **Prometheus 3.5.0 LTS** released July 2025 following the 3.0.0 major release in November 2024 provides React-based UI replacing Bootstrap, native histograms critical for ML latency tracking, OpenTelemetry compatibility, UTF-8 metric name support, and Remote Write 2.0 protocol. Grafana 11.0+ delivers Explore Metrics and Logs with query-less experiences streamlining dashboard creation. GPU monitoring via NVIDIA DCGM Exporter deployed as DaemonSets on GPU nodes provides DCGM_FI_DEV_GPU_UTIL for utilization percentages, DCGM_FI_DEV_FB_USED for memory usage in MB, DCGM_FI_DEV_GPU_TEMP for temperature in Celsius, DCGM_FI_DEV_POWER_USAGE in watts, and DCGM_FI_DEV_SM_CLOCK plus DCGM_FI_DEV_MEM_CLOCK for frequency monitoring.

## Edge deployments bring intelligence to the network periphery

Starlink Business delivers 40-350 Mbps download speeds with typical performance at 120-220 Mbps, 10-40 Mbps upload speeds typically 10-25 Mbps, and 20-50ms advertised latency with 25-60ms real-world measurements. The 2025 pricing structure changed substantially with initial equipment at $2,500 for business antenna, Priority 40GB plans at £80 UK or €114 EU monthly excluding VAT, Priority 1TB plans at £150 UK or €221 EU monthly, and $1 per GB overage charges with speeds throttling to 1 Mbps representing 99% reduction after priority data exhaustion. Power consumption reaches 50-100W continuous for standard antennas with estimated annual electricity costs of £110-220 for 24/7 UK operation.

For cell tower GPU vision workloads, **Starlink provides viable backup connectivity** rather than primary circuits. Upload bandwidth at 10-40 Mbps bottlenecks continuous video streaming, weather-dependent performance introduces variability, priority data caps require careful capacity planning, and shared bandwidth models lack dedicated guarantees. The solution suits edge-heavy processing with inference at towers and selective result uploads, backup connectivity to primary fiber or 5G, temporary deployments during site buildout, and rural or remote sites without alternatives. Edge inference with metadata and alert uploads only, rather than video streaming, represents the optimal use case.

CoreWeave operates **33 data centers** across North America and Europe with approximately 250,000 GPUs in early 2025 generating expected $5B revenue growing to $8-10B projected for 2026 following March 2025 IPO on Nasdaq. The distributed infrastructure provides hundreds of megawatts with liquid cooling in all 2025+ facilities, rack density up to 130kW, NVLink clusters with 100k+ GPU mega-clusters, and proprietary Mission Control orchestration software. While marketed as edge infrastructure, CoreWeave focuses on distributed low-latency data centers rather than true edge deployments at cell towers. The architecture suits centralized model training, large-scale inference clusters, cloud components of hybrid edge-cloud systems, and backup processing capacity but not micro-edge pods for tower installations.

**NVIDIA L40S Ada Lovelace GPUs** deliver optimal edge inference characteristics with 48GB GDDR6 with ECC memory, 4th generation Tensor Cores supporting FP8, 350W TDP manageable for edge power budgets, 18,000+ CUDA cores, up to 5x higher inference versus A40, excellent multimodal generative AI optimization, enterprise 24/7 data center design, NEBS Level 3 readiness, and secure boot with root of trust. Pricing at $30,000-40,000 per GPU positions L40S as the clear choice for cell tower deployments with power-efficient 350W envelopes, 48GB memory handling most vision models, excellent inference throughput, dual AI plus graphics capability, and enterprise reliability features.

H200 and Blackwell GPUs prove unsuitable for edge with H200's 700W TDP exceeding edge power budgets, requirements for advanced cooling infrastructure, overkill specifications for typical vision workloads, and better fit for centralized cloud inference. Blackwell B200/GB200 at 1,000-1,200W TDP completely exceeds edge capabilities requiring liquid cooling infrastructure, power draw surpassing edge site budgets, hyperscale data center design focus, and unsuitability for any edge use case. The performance per watt analysis confirms **L40S as excellent edge choice** versus H200's poor and B200's unsuitable ratings.

Edge orchestration with **K3s v1.28.2+** provides single binary under 100MB, embedded SQLite database or external etcd, minimal dependencies, full Kubernetes API compatibility, and CNCF certification. Resource requirements start at 512MB RAM minimum with 1GB+ recommended and 1 CPU core minimum with 2+ recommended, approximately 200MB disk for binary plus OS, and support for ARM64 and x86_64 architectures. The lightweight design delivers 40-50% less memory than full Kubernetes, fast installation via single curl command, built-in CoreDNS, Traefik, and ServiceLB components, HA support with embedded etcd, and excellent documentation. Management at scale integrates with Rancher for fleet management, GitOps via Flux or ArgoCD, multi-cluster deployments, and remote management capabilities.

MicroK8s 1.28 from Canonical tracks upstream Kubernetes releases with snap package distribution, Ubuntu-native integration, modular add-on system, zero-ops low-touch operations, and CNCF certification. Resource requirements reach 540MB RAM minimum with 2GB+ recommended and 1 CPU core minimum with 2+ recommended, approximately 500MB disk for snap package, and excellent ARM support. Features include automatic transactional updates with rollback capability, rich add-on ecosystem with 50+ add-ons, strictly confined security model, native clustering support, and GPU operator add-on availability.

**For cell tower deployments, K3s receives recommendation** for minimal resource footprint, maximum flexibility across operating systems, better ARM support for compact hardware, and integration with Rancher for fleet management. MicroK8s suits Ubuntu-based infrastructure with automatic update preferences and extensive add-on ecosystem requirements.

Power requirements for single L40S-based edge nodes total 500-650W IT load with GPU at 350W, CPU at 150-200W for Intel Xeon or AMD EPYC, memory, storage, and networking at 50-100W, cooling overhead at 20-25% of IT load producing 625-810W total with recommended 1,000-1,200W redundant PSUs. Cell tower power typically limits to 5-10kW total site budgets with GPU workloads consuming 20-40% of available capacity.

Cooling solutions for edge span **air cooling for 350-500W GPUs** suitable for L40S with proper front-to-back or rear-to-front airflow, ambient temperature under 30°C, dust filtration for outdoor environments, and products like Stulz CyberRack SideCooler delivering 18-51kW capacity at 0.7-2.6kW power consumption costing $15,000-30,000. Liquid cooling for 700W+ GPUs uses rear door heat exchangers as easiest retrofits, direct-to-chip cold plates on GPUs and CPUs, immersion cooling submerging servers in dielectric fluid, coolant distribution units, plumbing infrastructure, chillers or dry coolers, and monitoring systems costing $50,000-150,000+ with products like Alfa Laval Cooling Pods delivering 500kW-1MW in 20-foot containers at $100,000-200,000.

Cell tower recommendations favor **air cooling with L40S GPUs** as most practical approach using ruggedized weatherproof enclosures in compact 2U-4U server form factors with filtration critical for outdoor environments and remote monitoring essential. The configuration enables sub-10ms inference latency at edge, 99%+ bandwidth reduction versus cloud-only architectures, resilient operation during network outages, cost-effective scaling to hundreds of sites, and standards-based technology stacks ready for production deployment in November 2025.
