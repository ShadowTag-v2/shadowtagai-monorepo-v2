“I no longer take advisories, but I still review pitch decks weekly. GTM slides are cursed

it's technically correct to list all the partners, markets, regions, and acquisition channels. even better with some numbers around

but most teams tend to overcomplicate. the GTM slides should answer these questions:

how do you make money?
why should this work?
who's buying?
how can I (as an investor) be sure you'll make a few X(s) in a year from now?

assume you can't accompany this with a storytelling, and all partners, markets, and channels should be self-explanatory

GTM roadmap isn't static, and should be updated every month, if not weekly, in the active raising stage. every win counts, as it can shape future distribution. BDs can treat GTM as their sacred affirmations dream board, where even huge goals should be attainable in the long run

market and revenue projections are fundamental, but out of those promising trillions, only a few will sustain until the next year

many GTM slides lack realism. not only is the positioning "visionary"; teams list Aaves and Mophos of this world as their "partners" when they're just at the stage of knowing a guy who knows a guy

too many details make it hard to read, and template patterns as "CT lead", "10m giveaway in IG", "building strong community in Discord" are vaporwave

my humble advice on structuring GTM slights:

1. segment market projection, distribution channels, and GTM roadmap

2. be realistic in what's done and what you can achieve within the near-term timeline; generalize market hypotheses and use actionable structures, e.g., "testnet onboarding with key DeFi partners A, B, C ", or "using strategy XYZ for region N"

3. make sure that the GTM slides are strongly linked to your initial storytelling, and even without your comments, investors will recognize markets, audiences, and what you're selling

it's "go-to-market", baby. not "go-traumatize-me"”
