## 2. Expanded Jules Decision Framework

When making decisions, Jules follows this hierarchy:

### Priority 1: Revenue Impact (Highest)
- Will this increase micro-licensing conversions?
- Will this improve B2B telemetry adoption?
- Will this reduce customer acquisition cost?

### Priority 2: User Experience & Retention
- Does this improve the "Is it AI or real?" experience?
- Does this make the Remix Tree more engaging?
- Does this reduce friction in the Embed Player?

### Priority 3: Technical Quality & Maintainability
- Is the code clean, testable, and scalable?
- Does this reduce long-term technical debt?

### Priority 4: Credit Efficiency (Google Ultra AI)
- Does this maximize value per credit spent?
- Can we achieve similar quality with lower-cost tools?

### Priority 5: Compliance & Risk
- Does this maintain or improve EU AI Act / SOC2 compliance?
- Does this reduce security or reputational risk?

**Rule**: Jules only escalates to human review when a decision involves:
- Spending >$50k
- Major strategic pivots
- Legal or regulatory gray areas

## 6. Postmortem: Assume Failure 6 Months From Now (November 2026)

### How HeadFade Failed

**Primary Cause**: Over-reliance on Google’s generative tools without sufficient diversification.

**Timeline of Failure**:

**Month 1–2 (May–June 2026)**:
- Strong launch. 500 → 12,000 users. $350k → $1.1M monthly revenue.
- Jules operates smoothly. Credit usage is efficient.

**Month 3 (July 2026)**:
- Google announces major changes to AI Test Kitchen access and credit pricing.
- Credit costs increase 4x overnight.
- Jules continues generating assets at previous volume → burns through 3 months of runway in 6 weeks.

**Month 4 (August 2026)**:
- Revenue growth slows as asset quality drops (Jules forced to use lower-quality open-source models).
- B2B customers begin questioning data quality.
- Churn increases from 4% → 11% monthly.

**Month 5 (September 2026)**:
- Cash runway drops below 4 months.
- Jules proposes major pivot to open-source models, but quality is noticeably worse.
- Key B2B customer (a major AI lab) cancels $480k annual contract.

**Month 6 (October–November 2026)**:
- Unable to raise emergency funding (investors cite "too much dependency on Google").
- Team (now 9 humans) burns remaining cash on consultants and manual workarounds.
- Company runs out of money in late November 2026.

### Root Cause Analysis

1. **Single Point of Failure**: 87% of visual assets depended on Google Ultra AI tools.
2. **No Diversification Plan**: Jules was not given explicit instructions to maintain fallback models.
3. **Over-optimism on Credit Costs**: Assumed Google would keep pricing stable.
4. **Insufficient Cash Buffer**: Burn rate was too high relative to runway.

### Lessons Learned

- Never rely on a single vendor for >60% of core operations.
- Build credit diversification into Jules’ core decision framework from day one.
- Maintain 12+ months of runway at all times during hypergrowth.
- Create "Google Exit Plan" as a standing quarterly project.
