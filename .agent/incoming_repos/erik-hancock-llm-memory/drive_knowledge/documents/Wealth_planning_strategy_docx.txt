2. Legal/IRS Constraints (Where It Gets Risky)
The IRS has rules around “prohibited transactions” that can blow up a Roth IRA if mishandled.
Self-Dealing:
If you contribute founder stock after formation, it may count as a prohibited transaction (since you’re a “disqualified person” dealing with your own IRA). That can disqualify the entire account.
Valuation Issues:
The IRS requires assets transferred into Roths to have a fair market value (FMV). At formation, that’s basically $0.001/share. But if you understate value or move them in after incorporation, it can trigger penalties.
Control Problem:
If your Roth owns the company, you (personally) can’t provide services to it without risking prohibited transaction treatment. That makes “founder inside Roth” very tricky because you’re the one running the company.

3. Where Roths and Founders Do Work
There are cleaner paths that avoid prohibited transaction issues:
At Incorporation:
If the Roth IRA itself is the shareholder from day 1, and you don’t personally own those shares, then it can legally hold founder stock. But this means the IRA, not you, is the owner.
Through a Self-Directed IRA (SDIRA):
You’d need a custodian that allows private stock. The IRA makes the purchase at incorporation.
Spouse Option (Wife’s Roth):
Since she’s not a disqualified person (until you co-mingle), her Roth IRA could potentially hold founder stock more cleanly. But her age + time horizon make that a massive tax-free growth engine if it works.

4. Where It Breaks
If you personally contribute services to a company owned by your Roth → IRS could treat it as prohibited self-dealing.
If IRS challenges the valuation as “artificially low,” they may argue it’s an excess contribution.
Once the company grows, you can’t just transfer stock into a Roth anymore — FMV is too high.

5. The Safer Hybrid (What Most Do Now)
Layer Roth Exposure Early: Use Roths for small allocations of early preferred or seed shares at a legitimate third-party valuation. This avoids the “you formed it” issue.
Load Trusts with Founder Stock: Use GRAT/IDGT/Dynasty Trust for the big founder tranche. You retain operational control, avoid self-dealing rules, and can borrow vs. assets.
