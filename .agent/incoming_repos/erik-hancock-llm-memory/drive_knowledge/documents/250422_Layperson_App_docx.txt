2. Does This Solve the “Tort Reform” Gap?
Tort-reform has made negligence or personal-injury claims harder and less lucrative at the sub-$20 K level, leaving many small disputes unlitigated. Your app’s “verbatim-transcript → contract” approach:
Shifts disputes into pure contract-breach claims under small-claims jurisdiction
Bypasses the need to plead negligence, relying instead on the literal words exchanged

⚠️ Challenges
Judicial burden: small-claims judges may struggle to interpret raw, unedited transcripts (hearsay? admissibility?)
Evidence issues: audio/video quality, authenticity, completeness of transcript
User error: laypersons may mislabel informal negotiations or omit essential terms (price, scope)
Alternative counsel: even small-claims litigants sometimes hire “document preparers” or limited-scope attorneys
Overall, the app partially addresses the tort-reform gap by simplifying contract formation, but it does not fully eliminate the need for legal clarity or evidentiary rigor in court.

3. Unauthorized-Practice-of-Law Risk
Whether offering this app constitutes “practicing law without a license” depends on jurisdictional ethics rules. In most U.S. states:
Permissible:
Providing generic, standardized forms or assembly tools based strictly on user-supplied content
Including plain-language disclaimers stating the tool is not a substitute for legal advice
No “tailored advice” or “interpretation” of legal rights
Prohibited:
Drafting or revising content based on specific user facts (“Given your situation, I recommend…” )
Offering automated legal analysis or strategy (“You should sue for X, not negligence”)
Best practices to avoid UPL
Disclaimers: Clear statement that “This tool provides document assembly only; consult an attorney for legal advice.”
Neutrality: Don’t guide users on whether or how to enforce or negotiate terms.
Limitation of liability: Terms of service that emphasize user responsibility for accuracy and legal sufficiency.
If you adhere to a “self-service document assembly” model with robust disclaimers and no tailored guidance, the app is generally considered a non-law-practice software tool, not the unauthorized practice of law—though opinions vary slightly state to state.
